A Different Approach to Education Reform

We’ve all heard it before from students, “Time to cram for the test! Then afterwards I will forget everything that we had just learned.” This is an approach most students take at their personal education. Each class feels like a barrier that needs to be overcome. Once the mountain of barriers have been scaled, that is when one can finally get started with their life. Admittedly, I too go into most classes with the same attitude. Going from class to class with this mindset has felt wrong. Shouldn’t school be a place for learning and growth? Not a place of unnecessary hurdles? Going into writing this paper I have done a considerable amount of research on the idea. Initially I supposed all the blame could be given to the traditional testing and grades that we have been using for nearly a century.

I have concluded testing and grades are actually a necessary way of measuring a students progress. “This makes it easy for students to understand their grades and calculate the scores they need to achieve the mark they desire(“Why letter grades”).” If the traditional methods of measuring a students progress are necessary then how do we shift the students focus away from that measurement and more towards really learning in the classroom. I believe there is a key aspect missing from every classroom that, if added, will completely change a student's focus. That key aspect is there is an insufficient instruction of WHY a student needs to learn each topic. Currently a high school student is told to take a math class, an English class, a history class and a science class each year. Why? Well to fulfill the requirement.

What if at the beginning of each class instructors went through the benefits of taking their class. “Students’ intrinsic motivation is evident when they desire to learn simply because it interests them or they recognize the importance of learning(“Importance of high expectations”).” In math and science we could help students understand that these classes are required to help them develop a problem solving mind, learn how the world works around us, and to empower the student to use the content taught to be used in the real world. Not just the classroom. The main concept behind this concept is setting an expectation high enough to naturally motivate the student to learn because they want to learn. Not because they need to get a good grade. Although that is necessary.

Another aspect that needs to be emphasized is the importance of creating project based assignments wherever possible rather than giving students busy work. Where possible this mode of learning will move away from the mindset of memorize, then test. Naturally this will also help topics that usually focus on teaching concepts move towards teaching applicable content. As said by a well respected changemaker, Will Richardson, “Look, at the end of the day the only learning that really matters is the learning that you can apply(Richardson).” Typically math classes are solely concept based with a few application problems along the way. Imagine the benefits involved if an algebra student was given a project to find objects around their house to then calculate the different equations it would take to recreate the object on a graph. Sound complicated? Learning opportunities usually are complex by nature which allow the student to experience the trial and error process of solving a problem on their own.

Personally, I teach an introduction to programming class at a local technology college and each assignment is project based. Not a lot of time is spent lecturing the concept. To help the student truly understand we give the student problems to apply what they have learned. Not just that but each lesson builds on top of the other. This allows the student to naturally learn a skill. In the traditional classroom students often must memorize concepts that are difficult to relate to each other. Lesson after lesson students must do this. Truly grasping knowledge is difficult in such a mode.

Lastly, if we are able to move to a project based style of learning. Most assignments could now be considered pass/fail. “There is no such thing as a C or a D for a lazily written term paper. There is no failing. The only goal is to learn the material.(Spenver).” Instead a standard is set for each project and the student either sufficiently meets the criteria or does not. This would encourage students to give their best work. There will no longer be the option to settle for less.

Some critiques may say that school has been around for a long time and has evolved into something that works best for our day today. In contrast I would say that society is forever evolving and the education system simply is not evolving fast enough to remain relevant. To keep the education system relevant it must also continue to evolve as we continue to progress as a society.

In conclusion, I do not believe that the current system is a failing system. I simply believe, like any good thing, the education system can make great improvements if the individual student is the main focus. A higher standard needs to be set. Not a standard based on the grades a student achieves but a standard that elevates and empowers a student to really truly learn. Not earn grades. But to earn knowledge. In the words of Einstein, “Try not to become a man of success but rather try to become a man of value.”
