The History of Education Reform

Humans are naturally very adaptive and innovative creatures. Since the beginning of human history we have been getting better at teaching each other to survive and to adapt to our environment. Many years ago school was once a hunting lesson. To learn how to survive. Over many years we have become so advanced that hunting is not the main lesson to be taught. Instead we teach each other how to build tall structures, how to manipulate complicated data, how we can learn from the great lessons of the past, and so much more! With this innovation there is much competition on who can create the best environment for people to learn in. In recent years the United States education system has had much attention drawn to it as a system that may be falling behind compared to its competition.

In the 1970’s this was a topic that started to gain some traction in the United States Federal Government. Many worried that the United States no longer was producing the best of the best from its education system. What came forth was a movement called, “Back to Basics.”(Education Reform Timeline) As this movement gained traction many states implemented a “minimum competency test to assure that high school students master basic reading, writing and math skills.”(Education Reform Timeline) Around the same time the Federal Government took their own action and implemented standardized tests to better measure school performance (Education Reform Timeline). These reforms started a movement that continues on to this day.

For the next 30 years the movement to improve the education system will gain a lot of traction. By 2002 President George W. Bush took matters into his own hands by signing the No Child Left Behind Act. The idea behind this act was a standard would be made for each state that each student would need to hit in math and reading. To accomplish this goal many standardized tests were created to measure each state's growth. With these tests, some schools' overall funding became attached to the students test results. Putting teachers' jobs on the line if their students were not passing. A direct consequence of this was changing the curriculum in classrooms to be more of a testing study group rather than to really learn and understand the material(Klein).

States started to take action into their own hands and create what we now know today as “Common Core.” Common Core was a curriculum developed to help students come out on top. The main takeaway was that everyone would have the same curriculum. Math and English would be taught the same way in every single classroom. Once put into practice it was determined after a few years that students were not improving. In fact “low-performing students, who were supposed to benefit most, especially struggled.”(Goldstein)

Many states argued that this shift in curriculum was a negative impact on the education system which resulted in those states undoing Common Core from their curriculum. After nearly a decade of mass testing from the federal government President Obama signed the “Every Student Succeeds Act.”(Williams) The purpose of this act was to completely rewrite the prior “No Child Left Behind Act.” More power was given to the states to determine the best approach to measure success(Williams).

At the time of this writing the Every student Succeeds Act is still the active law in effect for the American Education System. This law continues to have a focus on testing to measure the progress of students, schools, and overall each state. Along with creating academic standards, each state must create a plan that involves “helping disadvantaged students”. Including students who are, in poverty, a minority, receive special education services, and have limited English language skills.

Today school systems have been encountering a large amount of change in the recent pandemic. All schools were forced to move to an online classroom which brought on a learning curve for all. Schools had to adapt quickly with very little notice. Since the schools were not prepared to move to an online classroom many schools offered students to accept a “P” on their transcript rather than to be given a normal letter grade. This “P” stands for “Pass.” If a student had at least a D- they could opt in to get a “P” instead. Creating a wrinkle in the traditional mode of grading. Many wonder what the long term effects the pandemic and online learning will have on the current generation of students. Will colleges accept a “P” on a students transcript?

All in all, one must ask themselves whether or not the education system is moving in a positive direction. Are the changes to have more standardized testing helping the individual student? What can we learn from the recent pandemic going forward? Should we make more changes and encourage education reform? Or is the current system the best system for our time?
